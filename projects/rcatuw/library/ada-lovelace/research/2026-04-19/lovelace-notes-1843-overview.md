# Overview of the Document

In 1842, the Italian military engineer Luigi Federico Menabrea published in French
a paper titled "Notions sur la machine analytique de M. Charles Babbage" in the
"Bibliothèque Universelle de Genève" (October 1842, No. 82). It was the first
published account of Babbage's Analytical Engine.

Ada Lovelace, at Babbage's urging, translated Menabrea's French memoir into English
for Taylor's Scientific Memoirs. Babbage then suggested she append her own
explanatory notes. She did — and her Notes ultimately ran to roughly three times
the length of Menabrea's original paper.

Published over her initials "A.A.L." in September 1843.

## Structure

- **Menabrea's memoir (translated by AAL)**: Descriptive account of the Analytical
  Engine, its card-based control, its ability to perform arithmetic operations
  mechanically, and the distinction between the "store" (memory) and the "mill"
  (processor) — though those terms as used today come from later Babbage writings.

- **Note A**: On the general distinction between the Difference Engine and the
  Analytical Engine; her framing of the Analytical Engine as a machine that
  "weaves algebraical patterns" — contains her most philosophical prose.

- **Note B**: On the role of variables and the notation of memory "cards"; the
  treatment of symbols as independent from their current numerical values.

- **Note C**: On cycles and "backing" — reusing the same cards by re-feeding
  them through the machine (anticipating the concept of the loop).

- **Note D**: On a worked example involving two equations in two variables,
  illustrating how the engine handles algebraic operations step by step.

- **Note E**: On trigonometrical and more complex function evaluation.

- **Note F**: On the elimination of variables from sets of linear equations —
  demonstrating how the engine could tackle problems beyond simple arithmetic.

- **Note G**: The longest and most famous note. Contains:
  1. Her disclaimer that the engine "has no pretensions whatever to originate
     anything" — only what it is ordered to do.
  2. Her tabular "Diagram for the computation by the Engine of the Numbers of
     Bernoulli" — a step-by-step plan of operations for computing Bernoulli
     numbers on the Analytical Engine. Widely described as "the first computer
     program." The diagram enumerates operation numbers, operation types
     (× ÷ + −), variables acted upon, indication of the variables receiving
     results, statement of the results of each operation, and indication of
     changes in value.

## Signature

Signed "A.A.L." — Augusta Ada Lovelace. She was, per Victorian custom and
her own preference, not named on the published paper.
